%% Abstract
\abstract{%
Large language model (LLM) serving stacks maximize single-node throughput but underperform when multiple GPUs differ in speed, queueing, or memory headroom. We present \textbf{DIO}, a non-invasive \emph{cluster control plane} that sits in front of unmodified OpenAI-compatible engines (e.g., stock vLLM). DIO learns per-worker ms/token slopes online with dual-timescale Normalized Least Mean Squares (NLMS) using $O(1)$ arithmetic per completion, ranks workers by a joint cost (service estimate, queue wait, tier, VRAM pressure, cache affinity), and applies Roofline-inspired hard blocks without trusting absolute latency predictions for admission. The system is released as \texttt{dio-serve}, a pip-installable gateway and library.

\textbf{Claim scope.} Under fixed hardware, DIO's contribution is \emph{relative cost ranking} for routing, not point-accurate millisecond prediction. On dual Tesla T4 GPUs with Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct and stock vLLM, multi-seed evaluation ($n{=}10$) shows that when one peer is delay-throttled ($\times 2$ observed e2e), NLMS reduces p99 end-to-end latency by $48.3\%\pm0.7\%$ versus Round-Robin and outperforms a $d{=}2$ RLS predictor; when both GPUs are identical, strategies are nearly tied---as expected. Absolute MAPE remains high ($\sim$90--130\%). Multi-SKU physical fleets are reported as ongoing validation (Section~\ref{sec:multisource}).
}

\keywords{LLM serving, cluster scheduling, heterogeneous GPUs, NLMS, load balancing, vLLM, open-source systems}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

LLM inference is increasingly deployed as a \emph{cluster service}: multiple OpenAI-compatible engines behind a gateway, often on mixed GPU generations or with unequal background load. Single-node systems such as vLLM~\cite{vllm}, SGLang~\cite{sglang}, and TGI~\cite{tgi} optimize continuous batching and KV-cache layout, but provide little request-granularity routing across workers. External balancers (Round-Robin, connection count, Kubernetes/Ray defaults) treat workers as interchangeable and request costs as uniform. In heterogeneous clusters this causes head-of-line blocking, idle fast GPUs, and memory-unsafe assignments that risk KV thrashing or OOM~\cite{nexussched,dean2013tail}.

We design \textbf{DIO} (Distributed Inference Orchestrator) as a thin control plane for this setting. Unlike engine-invasive two-layer schedulers~\cite{nexussched} or static multi-SKU capacity tables~\cite{melange}, DIO:
\begin{enumerate}
\item learns a scalar latency model per worker online with dual-timescale NLMS~\cite{haykin2002adaptive};
\item routes with a joint cost that couples predicted service time to queueing, tier capability, VRAM pressure, and prefix affinity;
\item decouples \emph{ranking} from absolute admission thresholds when prediction error is large;
\item deploys as an OpenAI-compatible front door (\texttt{dio-serve}) without patching engines.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Contributions (methods and evaluation scope).}
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Dual-timescale NLMS ranking signal.} Per-worker fast/slow slopes and bias with $O(1)$ updates; absolute MAPE is reported and is not treated as a success metric.
\item \textbf{Joint cost and Roofline-style safety.} Scalar score $S_w$ with soft VRAM penalties and hard exclusion of unsafe workers; admission modes that do not rely on untrustworthy $\hat{y}$ magnitude.
\item \textbf{Cluster software artifact.} Open-source Go reference plane and Python \texttt{dio-serve} library wrapping stock vLLM/compatible servers; measured scheduling overhead of order $10$--$15\,\mu$s.
\item \textbf{Multi-seed cluster evaluation.} Homogeneous dual-T4 validation, delay-proxy slope skew on real engines, controlled simulations, ablations, and NLMS vs.\ RLS under a fixed cost function. A multi-SKU physical campaign is in progress (Section~\ref{sec:multisource}).
\end{enumerate}
Numeric outcomes appear only in Section~\ref{sec:evaluation}.

\section{Background and related work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Single-node engines}
vLLM~\cite{vllm} popularized PagedAttention and continuous batching. SGLang~\cite{sglang} improves structured decoding and prefix reuse. TGI~\cite{tgi} targets production APIs. Disaggregated prefill/decode systems~\cite{distserve,splitwise,bullet} optimize pipeline stages. These systems excel on one node; multi-worker routing is external.

\subsection{Cluster orchestrators}
Ray Serve~\cite{rayserve} and Triton~\cite{triton} emphasize actors and model repositories with coarse metrics. Orca~\cite{orca} advances iteration-level batching on homogeneous clusters. AlpaServe~\cite{alpaserve} and CoCoServe~\cite{CocoServe} focus on placement and multiplexing. M\'elange~\cite{melange} targets heterogeneous capacity tables. NexusSched~\cite{nexussched} combines predictive routing with engine co-design and multi-feature predictors ($O(d^2)$ updates). Llumnix~\cite{llumnix} migrates live KV state. LoongServe~\cite{loongserve} targets long-context elastic parallelism. DIO complements these as a \emph{non-invasive request-level front end}: dual-timescale scalar NLMS, joint cost, and stock engines only.

\subsection{Adaptive prediction}
NLMS stability and step-size practice follow adaptive filter theory~\cite{haykin2002adaptive}. Queue wait uses a batch-amortized Little-style heuristic~\cite{littleslaw}. SJF-style ranking motivates predicted service routing~\cite{sjf}. Roofline~\cite{williams2009roofline} inspires soft memory ceilings recast as routing penalties.

\section{System design}
\label{sec:design}

\subsection{Architecture}
DIO separates a \textbf{control plane} from a \textbf{data plane} of unmodified engines (Fig.~\ref{fig:architecture}). Clients send OpenAI-compatible \texttt{/v1/chat/completions} to DIO. The scheduler selects a backend, proxies the request, and updates per-worker models from measured end-to-end latency and token counts. Product path: \texttt{dio-serve} (FastAPI + Python scheduler). Research path: Go manager with gRPC workers (optional).

\subsection{Dual-timescale NLMS latency model}
\label{sec:nlms}
For worker $w$ and approximate token count $N$, we model
\begin{equation}
\hat{y} = s_w N + b_w,
\label{eq:model}
\end{equation}
where $s_w$ is ms/token and $b_w$ is fixed overhead. On completion with residual $e = y - \hat{y}$, NLMS updates
\begin{equation}
s \leftarrow s + \mu \frac{e}{N}, \qquad
b \leftarrow b + \mu_b e,
\end{equation}
with dual rates $\mu_{\mathrm{fast}}{=}0.1$, $\mu_{\mathrm{slow}}{=}0.01$, $\mu_b{=}0.005$, and
\begin{equation}
s^{\mathrm{eff}} = \alpha s^{\mathrm{fast}} + (1-\alpha) s^{\mathrm{slow}}, \quad \alpha{=}0.8.
\label{eq:dual}
\end{equation}
Cold-start priors are $s_0{=}2.0$\,ms/token, $b_0{=}150$\,ms. We distinguish (i)~$O(1)$ \emph{arithmetic} per update from (ii)~time-to-usable ranking: typically \textbf{15--20 completions per worker} before slopes leave priors. Figure~\ref{fig:nlms_conv} illustrates dual-timescale response to an injected slope jump in simulation.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{fig_nlms_convergence_throttle.png}
\caption{NLMS dual-timescale adaptation under an injected true-slope jump at request 80 (simulation). $s^{\mathrm{fast}}$ reacts first; $s^{\mathrm{slow}}$ lags. Labeled synthetic illustration, not dual-T4 wall-clock traces.}
\label{fig:nlms_conv}
\end{figure}

Token feature $N$ prefers Hugging Face tokenizer counts plus planned \texttt{max\_tokens}; fallback is $\lfloor|\mathrm{prompt}|/4\rfloor+\texttt{max\_tokens}$.

\subsection{Joint cost routing}
\label{sec:cost}
For each healthy worker $w$,
\begin{equation}
S_w = \mathrm{wait}_w + \hat{t}_w + \mathrm{tierCost}_{r,w} + \mathrm{vramCost}_w - \mathrm{cacheBonus}_w,
\end{equation}
with $\hat{t}_w = s^{\mathrm{eff}}_w N + b_w$, $\mathrm{wait}_w = (Q_w/B)\bar{t}_w$ ($B{=}8$), soft VRAM penalty when free VRAM ${<}4$\,GB, hard exclusion when free VRAM ${<}2.4$\,GB and $N{>}1000$, tier mismatch soft cost $500$\,ms (hard block for large-on-small), and optional $200$\,ms prefix-hash affinity bonus. Route to $\arg\min_w S_w$ in $O(|W|)$ time.

\paragraph{Admission vs.\ ranking.}
Because absolute MAPE is high (Section~\ref{sec:mape}), admission is \emph{not} primarily ``reject if $\min S_w > \mathrm{SLO}$'' on raw $\hat{y}$. Modes: (i)~\texttt{rank\_only} (VRAM/tier hard blocks only; default for routing A/B); (ii)~\texttt{empirical} (rolling observed latency percentiles); (iii)~\texttt{absolute} (legacy, diagnostic). Metrics track disagreement between absolute and active modes.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{DIO worker selection (simplified)}
\label{alg:dio}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require request $r$, workers $W$, admission mode
\State $N \leftarrow$ token feature$(r)$
\State $w^\star \leftarrow \bot$, $S_{\min}\leftarrow\infty$
\ForAll{$w\in W$ healthy and tier-feasible}
  \State $\hat{t}_w \leftarrow$ Predict$(w,N)$
  \If{VRAM hard-blocked$(w,N)$} \textbf{continue}
  \EndIf
  \State $S_w \leftarrow$ wait$+$ $\hat{t}_w$ $+$ tier $+$ vram $-$ cache
  \If{$S_w < S_{\min}$} $S_{\min}\leftarrow S_w$, $w^\star\leftarrow w$
  \EndIf
\EndFor
\If{$w^\star=\bot$ or admission rejects} \Return HTTP 503
\EndIf
\State \Return $w^\star$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Implementation}
\texttt{dio serve -b e0=URL0 -b e1=URL1} fronts stock engines. Strategies: \texttt{nlms}, \texttt{rls}, \texttt{round\_robin}, \texttt{least\_loaded}, \texttt{static}. Debug metrics expose slopes, MAPE, routes, and admission counters. Artifacts and scripts: workshop validation suite under the public repository.

\section{Experimental evaluation}
\label{sec:evaluation}

\subsection{Methodology}
\label{sec:method}

\paragraph{Regimes (non-interchangeable).}
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Regime A --- homogeneous dual-T4 (real):} 2$\times$ Tesla T4, stock vLLM, Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct, HF tokenizer, $\texttt{max\_tokens}{=}32$, $n{=}10$ seeds $\times$ 30 requests.
\item \textbf{Regime B --- legacy Locust pilot (secondary):} longer-decode ShareGPT/arXiv/Azure, single seed, different setup; absolute seconds not mixed with Regime A ms.
\item \textbf{Regime C --- slope skew:} (i) CPU multi-seed simulation $n{=}10$; (ii) real dual-T4 with e1 behind delay proxy $\times 2$, $n{=}10$.
\item \textbf{Regime D --- multi-SKU (ongoing):} physical mixed GPU types (Section~\ref{sec:multisource}).
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Baselines.}
Under the \emph{same} joint cost and the same engines: Round-Robin (RR), Least-Loaded (LL), and RLS ($d{=}2$, $\lambda{=}0.99$). Primary metrics: p50/p99 e2e mean$\pm$std with approximate 95\% CI half-widths; MAPE; fraction to backend e0 (unthrottled/fast). We do not claim a matched multi-node Orca or vLLM-distributed bake-off.

\paragraph{Simulation vs.\ real.}
``Sim'' denotes library slope-skew or calibrated mocks. ``Real'' denotes dual-T4 + stock vLLM. Delay-proxy Regime C uses real tokens with inflated observed e2e on one peer---not a second physical SKU.

\subsection{Control-plane overhead}
\label{sec:overhead}
On the T7 scalability microbench with many concurrent mock workers, instrumented scheduling completes in approximately $\mathbf{14\,\mu s}$ per request (worker scan, cost evaluation, prefix hash). Per-worker state is $\sim$256\,B; the control plane is not the bottleneck relative to LLM decode. Figure~\ref{fig:scalability} shows the control-plane stress plot (e2e p99 is dominated by synthetic worker delay).

\subsection{Multi-SKU physical fleets (future work)}
\label{sec:multisource}
Real-GPU slope skew in this paper uses identical Tesla T4 SKUs with a delay proxy (controlled service-time skew on real engines and tokens). Validating the same protocol on a \textbf{physical multi-SKU} pair (e.g., T4$+$L4) is left to future work; we do not claim cross-architecture fleets from the present dual-T4 data alone.

\section{Discussion and limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Multi-SKU fleets.} Physical mixed SKUs are future work (Section~\ref{sec:multisource}); current real skew is delay-proxy on identical T4s.
\item \textbf{Baseline breadth.} RR/LL/RLS on the same vLLM pair; not a full multi-node Orca/vLLM-distributed study.
\item \textbf{High MAPE.} Absolute prediction is poor; admission is ranking-/observation-based.
\item \textbf{Homogeneous p99.} NLMS does not beat RR when workers match; value appears under skew.
\item \textbf{Workload.} Primary multi-seed cells use short chat probes; concurrent ShareGPT multi-seed on dual-T4 is incomplete.
\item \textbf{Out of scope.} Pipeline-parallel/disaggregated PD co-design, multimodal split inference, and privacy-preserving routing.
\end{enumerate}

%%=============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

DIO is a practical cluster control plane for LLM inference: dual-timescale NLMS ranking, joint cost routing, admission decoupled from absolute $\hat{y}$, and open-source \texttt{dio-serve} over stock vLLM-compatible engines. Multi-seed dual-T4 evidence shows strong p99 gains under controlled service-time skew and no invented wins on homogeneous GPUs. We release this as a systems technical report / open preprint so others can reproduce and extend the stack; multi-SKU fleets and broader production baselines remain natural extensions.
